Motivation and Rationale

Thermal sensing is rapidly emerging as a transformative area of research, driven by the proliferation of affordable, low-resolution thermal infrared array sensors. Unlike traditional visible-spectrum cameras, which capture detailed visual information at the expense of privacy, or radio-frequency (RF) sensing technologies, which offer privacy but often lack spatial resolution, thermal sensors provide a unique and compelling middle ground. These sensors detect the heat signatures of people and objects without revealing identifiable visual details, making them ideal for privacy-preserving applications in both public and private spaces.

Recent advances have demonstrated the remarkable potential of thermal arrays. For instance, the TADAR system has shown that even a single commodity thermal array can achieve multi-person detection and ranging by transforming 2D heat pixel data into 3D spatial information. This capability extends the reach of thermal sensing beyond traditional modalities such as RADAR, LIDAR, SONAR, or WiFi-based sensing, enabling rich spatial and activity awareness while inherently safeguarding sensitive visual data.

The timing for this research is particularly opportune. Miniaturized infrared array sensors—such as FLIR Lepton modules, Melexis microbolometer arrays, and Meridian thermal sensors—have become increasingly accessible and easy to integrate into mobile and IoT devices. This accessibility is catalyzing a wave of innovation across diverse fields, including mobile computing, human-computer interaction, computer vision, robotics, and localization. Researchers are now exploring thermal sensing as a new modality for ubiquitous computing, unlocking applications that were previously unattainable.

Thermal sensing also excels in challenging environments where conventional sensors fall short, such as low-light or smoke-filled conditions. At the same time, it offers robust privacy protection in sensitive settings like homes, workplaces, and healthcare facilities. The growing interest in this field is reflected in a surge of recent research, including the Heat-Assisted Detection and Ranging (HADAR) technique, which was featured as a Nature cover story in 2023.

Given these exciting developments, the time is ripe to organize HotSense. Our goal is to consolidate ongoing efforts, foster interdisciplinary collaboration, and shape the future research agenda for thermal sensing in mobile and pervasive computing. By bringing together experts and enthusiasts from academia and industry, HotSense aims to accelerate innovation and drive impactful advances in this promising domain.

Call for Papers

The HotSense workshop aims to bring together researchers interested in thermal/infrared sensing – particularly low-cost, low-resolution thermal arrays – and their applications in mobile systems, HCI, robotics, and beyond. We invite original research papers in (but not limited to) the following areas:

  • Thermal-based gesture detection and human activity recognition
  • Pose estimation and multi-person tracking with low-resolution thermal imaging
  • Indoor localization and movement tracking using thermal/infrared sensors
  • Thermal-assisted depth sensing and spatial structure inference
  • Robotic perception and autonomous navigation enhanced by thermal vision
  • Material sensing and object classification using thermal signatures
  • Privacy-preserving sensing and surveillance using thermal data
  • Low-power and energy-efficient thermal sensing platforms and algorithms
  • Novel hardware designs and system software for thermal sensing
  • Applications in mobile health, smart homes, AR/VR, and smart cities
